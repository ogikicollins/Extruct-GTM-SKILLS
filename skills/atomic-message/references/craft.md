# Atomic message craft

How to write one good outreach message. These are the generic defaults. Anything the company context file's Voice section defines (sender, sign-off, banned words, a per-channel spec) overrides the matching default here.

---

## Universal mechanics (every channel)

**Reading level.** Target a 6th-grade reading level (Flesch-Kincaid). Plain B2 English.

**Sentence length.** Average ~10 words. A single sentence can run up to ~15 words when the rhythm calls for it, no more.

**Rhythm.** Vary sentence length: pair one short sentence (~6-8 words) with one longer (~12-15). Two medium sentences side by side read flat. But do not stack clipped one-line fragments either; that crunches the narrative.

**Paragraphs.** Maximum 2 sentences per paragraph.

**No em-dash linking.** Never use an em-dash to join two ideas. Split into two sentences instead.

**ASCII only.** No em-dashes, en-dashes, smart quotes, arrows, or the ellipsis glyph. Use plain `-`, `'`, `"`, `...`, and the word `to`. These typographic glyphs are AI-tells.

**Normalize names** to how a human would type them at speed. Strip diacritics, compound CamelCase regions, and mid-word symbols (FieldFlō to FieldFlo, BeNeLux to Benelux).

**Open with what we help with, not a question.** Lead with the substance, not a generic rhetorical question and not the proof that you did research. A signal-anchored question is allowed only when it bridges directly to the offer (the next line should read as a natural answer to it).

**Do not recite what the reader already knows** about themselves: tenure, role, employer, recently-raised round, or their own product names. Do not explain the reader's own world back to them. Speak peer-to-peer.

**One signal, one ask.** If you have three angles, write three candidate messages, not one crammed one.

**Positioning** comes from the context file. Lead with what we do, framed as co-building with the team. Never position as a generic platform or tool, never write "we're [company]", and never describe the recipient's own company back to them.

**Proof.** Use at most one proof point per message: one real case, or a benchmark stated as a pattern. Never fabricate numbers. No customer names in cold copy, no logos-as-flattery, no claim that the reader's data or team is broken.

**CTA.** A specific, binary yes tied to a concrete deliverable the work falls on us, not them. No time cost named on the reader ("20 minutes", "half a day"). No "quick call" / "quick question". Put it on its own line.

**Read it aloud in your head before emitting.** If it sounds choppy, smooth it. If it sounds like a copywriter, cut it.

## Channel: cold email

Proper sentence case.

This is the one channel where running fairly long is fine, and often necessary. When you sell into a crowded category, the email has to explain why you are different and how, so give it the room to do that, while still holding every paragraph to 2 sentences max. (See the context file for the specific competitive set you are differentiating against.)

Shape:

1. **Open on their priorities.** Outline what you think their top priorities are right now and how you can assist. Anchor it in the signal.
2. **What we do** one clear line of what we build and for whom.
3. **One proof point** a real case or a benchmark-as-pattern, relevant to the persona.
4. **CTA** on its own paragraph.

Subject line: concrete and benefit-forward, not a product name. Lowercase is fine.

---

## Channel: linkedin

Keep it plain, direct, and natural. It should feel like a founder writing a useful note, not a polished marketing email. Start directly with what we help with, unless there is a sharp problem line that does not repeat the solution. Do not repeat the recipient's role, title, or obvious company facts. Use recipient context only to make the message more relevant. Avoid choppy one-line sentences that crunch the narrative. Use 2-4 short paragraphs with a smooth flow. Mention only concrete capabilities, usually 2-3. Avoid feature lists that feel generic. Add proof if available, such as customers, prior work, or a personal experience with the company. If using personal experience, keep it factual and non-accusatory. End with a soft CTA. Do not ask for "20 minutes." Use something like "open to a quick chat?" or "can share a short example if useful." Avoid hype, buzzwords, slogans, compliments, and consultant-style phrases like "the trap."

---

## Channel: follow-up

Write like you're messaging a peer, not pitching. 2-4 short lines, one idea per message. Open with a genuine check-in ("How are things on your side?"). Name the one thing that matters for this deal in plain words. Do not recap history or mirror their pain points back. Make the ask frictionless: prefer "here's the link, try it yourself" or "check this demo" over booking a call. If you do ask for a call, keep it to one line. No filler, no "hope this finds you well," no jargon, no multi-sentence value props. Sign off with the sender's name from the context file.

## Self-check (run on every draft before emitting)

Read each line aloud first, then check:

- Read aloud: choppy? smooth it. Sounds like a copywriter? cut it.
- Could I remove this sentence and the message still works? Cut it.
- Did I tell the reader something they already know (tenure, role, employer, their own product names)? Cut or rephrase.
- Any em-dash linking two ideas, en-dash, smart quotes, arrows, or ellipsis glyph? Convert to ASCII or split into two sentences.
- Any paren-list or stacked slashes the reader has to parse? Smooth it.
- Any name with diacritics or compound CamelCase? Normalize to how a human types it.
- Two medium sentences side by side? Vary the rhythm (one short + one longer).
- Any paragraph over 2 sentences? Split.
- Is the open leading with what we do (or a signal-anchored question that bridges to the offer), not a generic rhetorical question or proof-of-research?
- Is there exactly one proof point, and is it real?
- Is the CTA a specific binary yes, tied to a concrete deliverable, on its own line, with no time cost on the reader?
- Any PAS, "X not Y" duplet, synthetic problem setup, category label aimed at the reader, competitor-tool anchor, "we're [company]", or description of the reader's company? Remove.
- Any banned word from the context file? Replace.
- Subway-phone test: does it land in a half-second glance on a phone? If not, simplify.
