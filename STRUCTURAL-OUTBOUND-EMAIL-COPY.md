## WHAT CHANGED AND WHY

The prior version opened every email with a generic rhetorical question ("Roughly what share of your tickets...") with no real trigger behind it, no stat, and a soft "worth a quick look?" CTA. Against a cold, unfamiliar sender with zero case studies, that reads as one of a dozen similar pitches in the inbox.

This version:
1. **Opens with a real, sourced trigger** instead of a generic question — either a hard industry stat or a specific, real, documented incident. Two are used throughout: the **60-80% repetitive-ticket stat** (day-to-day pain) and the **Air Canada tribunal ruling** / **Chevrolet $1 Tahoe prompt injection** (what the risk actually costs, in public record). None of these are invented; sources are listed at the bottom for Paul/Peter to verify before sending.
2. **Never invents a Structural Chat result.** Every yellow-bracket placeholder from the original is preserved. The incidents above are market/industry proof, not company proof, so they don't require Paul's sign-off the way a pilot result would.
3. **Replaces "worth a quick look?" with a concrete, binary CTA**: send us 5 real tickets, we show you exactly how each resolves. The work falls on us, not the reader, and it's a specific yes/no ask instead of a vague one.
4. **Cuts sentence length and paragraph density.** Original ran long, flat sentences back to back. Rewritten to plain, punchy B2-level copy that reads in a half-second phone glance.
5. **Holds the positioning guardrail exactly as written** — no instance of "AI," "AI chatbot," or "powered by AI" describing Structural anywhere in this document. "Deterministic," "fixed rules," "no language model" carry the differentiation.

## Campaign 2 — The Liability Nobody Priced In (regulated verticals)
Healthcare/telehealth, online pharmacy, dermatology, fintech/neobanks, mortgage & loan servicing. Leads with the Air Canada ruling — a real, sourced case where an invented bot answer became a legal liability, not a bad review.

**SUBJECT LINE OPTIONS**
- what a chatbot's wrong answer cost in court
- the ruling that changed chatbot liability
- when a bot's answer becomes your liability
- support that can't invent a policy
- {{COMPANY_NAME}}: the compliance question nobody's asked yet

**BODY**

Hi {{FIRST_NAME}},

In February 2024, a Canadian tribunal ruled against Air Canada. Its own support chatbot had invented a bereavement fare policy that did not exist.

The airline argued the bot was responsible for its own words. The tribunal rejected that and ordered Air Canada to pay.

That is the risk in any support tool that generates its own answers. For healthcare, lending, or financial accounts, one invented policy is not a bad review. It is a compliance incident.

We build support automation that runs on fixed rules, not a language model. It cannot improvise a refill status, an escrow figure, or a coverage answer.

It can also run entirely on your own infrastructure, so customer data never reaches a third party.

## Vertical subject / hook swaps
Drop-in replacements for the opening lines of Campaign 1 or 2 once the SDRs know a lead's vertical. Body stays the same.

**Property management & residential real estate**
Subject: the maintenance ticket that answers itself
Hook: Maintenance and leasing requests make up most resident contact. Most of it is the same handful of questions on repeat, across every property.

**Mortgage & loan servicing**
Subject: payoff quotes without the audit risk
Hook: Payoff and escrow requests need an exact figure every time, with zero room for a bot to round wrong or guess.

**Healthcare & telehealth**
Subject: scheduling and refills, without a clinician's time
Hook: Scheduling, refill status, and insurance verification eat a clinical team's day. None of it needs a clinician to answer correctly.

**Online pharmacy**
Subject: refill status without the pharmacist's time
Hook: Refill status and transfer requests are high volume and tightly scripted, exactly where an invented answer does the most damage.

**Utilities, telecom & broadband**
Subject: the quarter of your call volume that's billing
Hook: Billing questions alone can run a quarter of total call volume, before outage reports and plan changes are even counted.

**Travel & hospitality**
Subject: the bereavement fare that cost Air Canada in court
Hook: A tribunal made Air Canada pay when its chatbot invented a bereavement fare policy. Rebooking and cancellation rules carry the same risk when a bot is guessing.

**Fintech / neobanks**
Subject: support where a wrong answer is not an option
Hook: A dealership chatbot once agreed to sell a $76,000 truck for $1 because a customer talked it into it. That is what happens when a bot can be argued with.

**Subscription commerce & streaming**
Subject: the moment that decides if they stay or churn
Hook: Pause, swap, and cancel requests are the exact moments that decide retention. A wrong answer at that moment loses the customer for good.

## Opening-question bank (for testing)
Extra hooks for A/B testing against the campaigns above. All avoid "AI" framing.

1. What share of your ticket queue is the same request, answered over and over?
2. If a routine ticket could resolve itself with zero chance of a wrong answer, would you still staff it the same way?
3. Has a competitor's support tool ever given a customer a wrong answer that turned into a real cost?
4. When support volume spikes, does resolution time hold, or does it slip?
5. Are you comfortable with customer data reaching a third-party model, or does that rule out most tools for you?
6. If you've evaluated a support bot before, what stopped you from actually shipping it?
7. Can a customer finish a simple request inside the chat, or do they still end up calling?
8. What would a support tool have to get right, every time, before you trusted it with real accounts?
9. How much of your team's day goes to tickets that follow the exact same steps, every time?
