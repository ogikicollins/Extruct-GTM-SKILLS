## PART 8: THE SALES MOTION — DISCOVERY TO CLOSED/WON

### Stage 1: The Discovery Call (30–45 minutes)

Every discovery call follows the **SPIN + Pain Map** framework. The goal is not to pitch — it's to map the prospect's world and let the pain sell itself.

**Opening (2 min):**
> "Before I tell you anything about us, I want to make sure this is worth your time. Mind if I ask a few questions about where you are with [Atlassian / cloud / IT] right now?"

**Situation questions:**
- "How many people are using Jira/Confluence today?"
- "Are you on Server, Data Center, or Cloud?"
- "How big is your IT or DevOps team?"
- "Who owns the Atlassian admin responsibilities right now?"

**Problem questions:**
- "What's the biggest headache with your current setup?"
- "Has the Server EOL migration happened, or is it still pending?"
- "When something breaks, what does the escalation path look like?"
- "Are there things you know you should fix but just haven't gotten to?"

**Implication questions:**
- "What happens to engineering velocity when Jira is down or slow?"
- "What would it cost you if a migration goes wrong and you lose a week of productivity?"
- "Is the IT workload creating real pressure on your team right now?"

**Need-Payoff questions:**
- "If you had clean, fast Jira and someone managing the admin so your team didn't have to — what would that unlock for you?"
- "If the migration was behind you in 6 weeks, where would you focus the time back?"

**Close the discovery:**
> "Based on what you've told me, I think there's a clear fit here. I'd like to put together a short scope recommendation — not a 40-page proposal, just a clear picture of what we'd do, what it costs, and what you get. Can I get that over to you by [specific date]?"

---

### Stage 2: Qualify Before You Invest in a Proposal

Use the **MEDDIC framework** to score every opportunity before writing a proposal. Don't waste proposal time on deals that aren't real.

| Letter | What to Identify |
|--------|-----------------|
| **M** — Metrics | What does success look like in numbers? (migration timeline, uptime %, cost reduction) |
| **E** — Economic Buyer | Who actually signs? IT Director may need CFO or CEO approval |
| **D** — Decision Criteria | What matters most to them? (speed, price, certifications, references) |
| **D** — Decision Process | How many stakeholders? Security review? Legal? Procurement? |
| **I** — Identify Pain | Is the pain acknowledged, owned, and urgent — or theoretical? |
| **C** — Champion | Is there someone inside who wants you to win? |

**Disqualify fast if:**
- No clear budget owner identified
- Timeline is "sometime next year" with no forcing event
- Pain is real but nobody owns it internally
- They want you in a 5-vendor RFP with no incumbent advantage and no champion

---

### Stage 3: The Proposal That Closes

**Never send a generic PDF.** Structure every proposal as a **Decision Document** — written so the economic buyer can forward it internally and use it to get approval without you in the room.

**Proposal Structure:**

```
1. What We Heard        — Mirror their exact pain back in their words
2. What We Recommend    — Specific scope, not a menu of options
3. What It Costs        — Clear line items, no surprises
4. What Happens Next    — Week-by-week project plan with milestones
5. What Risks We Remove — ROI framing, cost of the problem continuing
6. What Happens If You Wait — Make the cost of inaction explicit
7. Next Step            — One CTA: sign and schedule kickoff
```

**Proposal delivery rule:** Never email a proposal cold. Walk through it live on a 30-minute call. Handle objections in the moment before they become silent deal-killers.

---

### Stage 4: Objection Handling — The Big 5

**"You're more expensive than the other quote."**
> "What's the other quote covering? Most quotes we see low-ball the scope, then the change orders hit. I'd rather show you exactly what's included so you can compare apples to apples. Can you share what the scope of that other proposal looks like?"

**"We need to think about it."**
> "Totally fair. What's the one thing that would make this a clear yes or a clear no? I'd rather help you make the right decision than follow up forever hoping you'll change your mind."

**"We might just handle this internally."**
> "What does that look like specifically? I ask because we see a lot of teams who start migrations internally and call us 6 weeks in when something breaks. I want to make sure you have a realistic picture of the effort before you commit to that path."

**"We don't have budget right now."**
> "When does your next budget cycle start? And is this the kind of thing you'd want to put in for — or is it genuinely not a priority for the business right now?" (Distinguish no-budget from no-priority — they're completely different problems.)

**"We already have an MSP."**
> "What does that relationship look like? We work alongside existing IT vendors all the time, especially when there's a specific Atlassian or cloud gap the current partner can't fill. What's not getting solved right now?"

---

### Stage 5: Closing Mechanics

**Trial close throughout every conversation:**
- After discovery: "Does what I described match what you're looking for?"
- After proposal walkthrough: "Does the scope feel right, or is there something you'd want to adjust?"
- At the close: "Based on everything we've talked through, is there anything standing between us and a kickoff date?"

**Create urgency honestly:**
- Project capacity is finite — be truthful about your calendar
- Migration complexity increases with delay (risk grows, not shrinks)
- Cloud costs are bleeding every month — quantify the number and put it in the proposal

**The Signature Moment:**
- Use DocuSign or PandaDoc — friction at the contract stage kills deals that were already won
- Include a scheduled kickoff meeting inside the proposal — signing automatically triggers a next step
