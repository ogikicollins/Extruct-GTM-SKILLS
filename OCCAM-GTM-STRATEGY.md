### Stage 2: ENGAGE — The First Conversation

Every first conversation follows the **SPIN + Pain Map** framework. The goal is not to pitch — it's to map the prospect's world and let the pain sell itself.

**The Discovery Call Framework (30–45 minutes):**

**Opening (2 min):**
> "Before I tell you anything about us, I want to make sure this is worth your time. Mind if I ask a few questions about where you are with [Atlassian / cloud / IT] right now?"

**Situation questions:**
- "How many people are using Jira/Confluence today?"
- "Are you on Server, Data Center, or Cloud?"
- "How big is your IT or DevOps team?"
- "Who owns the Atlassian admin responsibilities right now?"

**Problem questions:**
- "What's the biggest headache with your current setup?"
- "Has the Server EOL migration happened, or is it still pending?"
- "When something breaks, what does the escalation path look like?"
- "Are there things you know you should fix but haven't gotten to?"

**Implication questions:**
- "What happens to engineering velocity when Jira is down or slow?"
- "What would it cost you if a migration goes wrong and you lose a week of productivity?"
- "Is the IT workload creating pressure on your team right now?"

**Need-Payoff questions:**
- "If you had clean, fast Jira and someone managing the admin so your team didn't have to — what would that unlock for you?"
- "If the migration was behind you in 6 weeks, where would you focus the time back?"

**Close the discovery:**
> "Based on what you've told me, I think there's a real fit here. I'd like to put together a short scope recommendation — not a 40-page proposal, just a clear picture of what we'd do, what it costs, and what you get. Can I send that over by [date]?"

### Stage 3: QUALIFY — Is This a Real Opportunity?

Use the **MEDDIC framework** to score every opportunity:

| Letter | What to Identify |
|--------|-----------------|
| **M** — Metrics | What does success look like in numbers? (migration timeline, uptime %, cost reduction) |
| **E** — Economic Buyer | Who signs the contract? IT Director may not have final authority |
| **D** — Decision Criteria | What factors matter most? (price, speed, certifications, references) |
| **D** — Decision Process | How many people involved? Security review? Legal? Procurement? |
| **I** — Identify Pain | What's the specific, acknowledged pain? Is it urgent? |
| **C** — Champion | Is there someone inside actively helping you win? |

**Disqualify fast if:**
- No clear budget ownership
- Timeline is "sometime next year" with no forcing event
- Pain is acknowledged but not owned ("our CEO doesn't really care about this")
- They want you to compete in a 5-vendor RFP with no incumbent advantage

### Stage 4: CONVERT — Proposal to Closed/Won

#### The Proposal That Closes

**Never send a generic PDF proposal.** Structure every proposal as a **Decision Document** — written as if you're the economic buyer making the case to approve.

**Proposal Structure:**

```
1. What We Heard (mirror their pain back exactly)
2. What We Recommend (specific scope, not a menu)
3. What It Costs (clear line items, no surprises)
4. What Happens Next (week-by-week project plan)
5. What Risks We Eliminate (ROI framing)
6. What Happens If You Wait (cost of inaction)
7. Next Step (one CTA: schedule kickoff or sign)
```

**Proposal delivery rule:** Never email a proposal cold. Walk through it live on a 30-min call. Ask for questions in the moment. Handle objections before they become email threads.

---

#### Objection Handling — The Big 5

**"You're more expensive than the other quote."**
> "What's the other quote covering? Most quotes we see low-ball the scope, then the change orders hit. I'd rather show you exactly what's included so you can compare apples to apples. Can you share what the scope of that other proposal looks like?"

**"We need to think about it."**
> "Totally fair. What's the one thing that would make this a clear yes or a clear no? I'd rather help you make the right decision than follow up forever hoping you'll change your mind."

**"We might just handle this internally."**
> "What does that look like specifically? I ask because we see a lot of teams who try internal migrations and end up calling us 6 weeks in when something breaks. I want to make sure you have a realistic picture of the effort before you commit."

**"We don't have budget right now."**
> "When does your next budget cycle start? And is this the kind of thing you'd want to put in for — or is it genuinely not a priority for the business?" (Distinguish no-budget from no-priority)

**"We already have an MSP."**
> "What does the relationship look like? We work alongside existing IT vendors all the time, especially when there's a specific Atlassian or cloud gap the current partner can't fill. What's not getting solved right now?"

---

#### Closing Mechanics

**Trial close throughout the process:**
- After discovery: "Does what I described match what you're looking for?"
- After proposal walkthrough: "Does the scope feel right, or is there something you'd want to adjust?"
- At the close: "Based on everything we've talked through, is there anything standing between us and a kickoff date?"

**Create urgency honestly:**
- Project slots fill up — be truthful about your calendar
- Migration complexity increases with delay (Server EOL risk, data migration time)
- Cloud cost bleeding every month is quantifiable — show the number

**The Signature Moment:**
- Use DocuSign or PandaDoc for contracts — friction kills deals in final stage
- Include a kickoff meeting on the calendar inside the proposal so signing triggers a scheduled next step immediately

## PART 9: THE COMPETITIVE MOAT

**Layer 1 — Technical depth at personal scale**

**Layer 3 — The single POC model**
This is a genuine structural differentiator for 100–500 employee companies. They've been burned by ticket queues and offshore tier-1 support. The value of "you'll talk to the same expert every time" is worth a 20–30% price premium and dramatically lowers churn.
