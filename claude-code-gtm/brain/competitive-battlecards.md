## Card 1: Belkins / CIENCE / Managed SDR Services

**Their pitch:**
> "We're a fully managed SDR service. Real humans research every prospect, write personalized emails, and book meetings for you. Pay per month, we handle everything."

**Their strengths:**
- Done-for-you feels lower risk than a build
- Client doesn't need technical knowledge
- Accountability is clear: "we'll book X meetings per month"

**Their weaknesses:**
- Humans can't scale beyond ~35-40 personalized outreach actions/day per rep
- Quality degrades as they split attention across multiple clients
- They own the system — when you stop paying, the pipeline stops
- Average output: 8-15 meetings/month at $6K-10K/month = $400-1,000 per meeting
- No signal layer — outreach is calendar-triggered, not event-triggered

**When a prospect says:** "We're using / evaluating Belkins / CIENCE"

**What to ask first:**
> "How many meetings per month are you getting right now, and what are you paying per month?"

*Listen. Then:*

**The positioning response:**
> "So you're paying [$X]/month for [Y] meetings — that's roughly $[cost per meeting] per meeting. With SELLL, you're looking at 200+ daily conversations running automatically, and a human SDR handling the warm replies and relationship layer. Most clients see 25-40 meetings per month. The math usually looks like [run the ROI comparison]. The other difference: at 90 days, you own the system. When you stop working with Belkins, the pipeline stops. When you're done with the SELLL build, you own a machine that keeps running."

**The proof point to use:**
> Devolon — same team size, 200+ daily conversations vs. the 35 a managed SDR pod delivers manually.

**Competitive displacement signal:**
> "We're paying $8K/month for 5 meetings" — this is the breaking point. If they express this frustration, they're ready to switch.

## Card 2: Apollo.io

**Their pitch:**
> "All-in-one B2B intelligence and engagement platform. Prospecting data, email sequences, LinkedIn automation, and AI-powered outreach in one place."

**Their strengths:**
- Single platform — no integration complexity
- Large database (250M+ contacts)
- Lower cost ($79-$99/month per user)
- AI writing assistance built in
- Self-serve — no contract or implementation needed

**Their weaknesses:**
- Everyone uses Apollo — the same lists, the same sequences, the same AI-generated copy
- No real-time signal layer — filters are static (title, company size, industry)
- AI personalization is surface-level (LinkedIn headline + company name)
- No orchestration across channels — email, LinkedIn, and ads are separate
- No human judgment layer for warm replies
- Reply rates average 0.5-1% because the "personalization" is the same for everyone

**When a prospect says:** "We already use Apollo" or "How are you different from Apollo?"

**What NOT to say:** "Apollo is great but..." — don't position as better than them.

**The positioning response:**
> "We actually use Apollo as one of our data sources — it's a great database. The difference is what sits on top of it. Apollo sends your email when you add someone to a sequence. The SELLL system sends your email when a specific event happens at that company: they raised funding last week, their VP Sales just started, they posted on LinkedIn about pipeline problems. Same message, right person, but triggered by a signal — not a filter. That's why our clients see 3-5% reply rates on the same type of outreach Apollo users get 0.5% on."

**Visual framing (for discovery calls):**
```
APOLLO: Database → Filter → Sequence → Send
SELLL:  Database × 45 → Signal Detection → Right Timing → Personalized Send → Coordinated Channels
```

**The proof point:**
> 3-5% reply rate vs. 0.5-1% industry average on signal-triggered outreach.

## Card 3: Refine Labs / Demand Generation Agencies

**Their pitch:**
> "Dark social, inbound demand generation, and content-driven pipeline. Buyers find you rather than you chasing them. Sustainable, brand-safe, long-term growth."

**Their strengths:**
- Inbound pipeline is high quality (buyer is already warm)
- No risk of damaging brand with aggressive outbound
- Content compounds over time
- Aligns with how modern B2B buyers research (they do 70% research before contacting sales)

**Their weaknesses:**
- Timeline: 6-12 months before inbound generates consistent pipeline
- No control over timing — you can't make inbound spike when the board asks for pipeline this quarter
- Dependent on content volume and SEO — requires ongoing investment
- Doesn't solve the "we need meetings next month" problem

**When a prospect says:** "We're focused on inbound / dark social / content"

**The positioning response:**
> "Inbound is a great long-game investment — we're actually building LinkedIn content into the engine too. The challenge is timeline: solid inbound takes 6-12 months to generate consistent pipeline. Outbound generates meetings in 30 days. Most successful companies run both simultaneously — and the intelligence layer is what makes them compound. Your outbound accounts get retargeted by your ads. Your LinkedIn content warms the prospects you're in sequence with. They reinforce each other rather than competing."

**The question to ask:**
> "What does your inbound pipeline look like right now — how many meetings per month is it generating, and how long has the strategy been running?"

*If they say "it's early" or "we're still building" — they know it's not working yet. That's your opening.*

**Proof point to use:**
> Any client where outbound + content combined drove faster results than either alone.

## Card 4: Kalungi / GTM Consulting / Fractional VP Sales

**Their pitch:**
> "Experienced GTM operators embedded in your team as fractional leaders. Strategic and tactical — we diagnose the problem and build the playbook. Expert-led."

**Their strengths:**
- Senior expertise on-demand without full-time cost
- Trusted advisor relationship
- Strategy-first = no wasted execution
- Flexible — can scale up or down

**Their weaknesses:**
- Advisory by nature — delivers recommendations, not a working system
- Consulting output is a document: an ICP, a playbook, a framework
- Client still has to implement — and most implementations stall
- No ongoing execution or automation layer
- Monthly cost is similar to SELLL ($5K-15K/month) but output is advisory, not operational
- When engagement ends, the knowledge walks out with the consultant

**When a prospect says:** "We have a fractional CRO / GTM consultant" or "We worked with a consulting firm"

**The positioning response:**
> "What did they deliver at the end of the engagement — a strategy document or a working system?"

*Most will say a document. Then:*

> "That's exactly the pattern we see most often: great intelligence in the document, no implementation path. SELLL is the execution arm — we take the intelligence and build a system that runs. If you already have a playbook from your consultant, that actually speeds up our intelligence phase. We'd be building on work that's already done."

**The proof point to use:**
> Holz Concepts / Stefan Golz — came in with a mandate (had done some strategy), needed execution. SELLL built the system, he hit targets.

**H7 hypothesis applies:** Companies that engaged a consultant and got a plan but no execution are among the highest-conversion prospects — they're pre-sold on the problem and pre-sold on the need for outside help.

## Card 5: Clay / DIY Stack Builders

**Their pitch (their users' pitch):**
> "I'm going to build this myself with Clay + Apollo + Instantly + ChatGPT. Full control, lower cost, exactly what I want."

**Their strengths:**
- Full control over every component
- Can be cheaper if someone internal has the skills
- Technically sophisticated buyers feel empowered
- No vendor dependency

**Their weaknesses:**
- Build time: 6-9 months to hire the right people (Clay expert, email copywriter, RevOps) and actually ship something that works
- Clay expertise is scarce and expensive ($80-120K/year for a good Clay specialist)
- Maintenance burden is ongoing — every tool change requires a system update
- Most "we'll build in-house" decisions stall after 3 months and never ship
- Time cost: the 6-9 months of delayed pipeline is worth far more than the build cost

**When a prospect says:** "We're thinking about building this in-house with Clay"

**The positioning response:**
> "Totally valid — Clay is a powerful tool and we actually use it in the stack. The question is timeline and opportunity cost. To build what we build in-house: you're looking at 6-9 months to hire a Clay specialist (they're rare and run $80K+), another 30-60 days of setup, and 30 days of testing before the first campaign goes live. That's 9-12 months of delayed pipeline. Our 90-day build gets you the same outcome — and you own the code and infrastructure at the end. You can hire into it afterward and take it over completely."

**The follow-up question:**
> "Who internally would own the build? Do you have someone with Clay expertise today?"

*Most won't. That's the moment to close.*

**Proof point:**
> Timeline comparison: 90 days (SELLL) vs. 9-12 months (internal build). At their ACV and their current pipeline gap, quantify the cost of those 6-9 extra months.
