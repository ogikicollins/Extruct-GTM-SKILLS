## 1. Executive Summary

TopServ sells a $5K–$10K/mo "Brandformance" retainer (plus $10K activation, plus ad spend) to home service contractors doing $1.5M–$5M/year who are tired of paying an ever-rising tax to Google for leads that don't compound into brand equity. Their previous cold-email motion failed; their referral/reputation channel works but doesn't scale. That gap — a proven high-ticket offer with no scalable acquisition channel — is exactly what a phone-first outbound motion is built to close, and it lines up with TopServ's own belief that this audience answers phones, not email.

The strategy below has three layers:

1. **Market entry** — a tightly scoped ICP and messaging system that gets the first 5 meetings/month fast enough to clear the scale gate.
2. **Operating system** — the cadence, data, and objection infrastructure that makes those 5/month repeatable and then multipliable once headcount scales.
3. **Market dominance** — using this engagement as the flywheel for TopServ's ~300-agency referral network, turning one client's outbound motion into a category position ("the outbound engine for brand-first home service agencies") that compounds well beyond this single account.

## 3. Positioning & Messaging Framework

TopServ does not sell marketing services — it sells **market authority**. Every message, script, and email needs to reframe the prospect's problem from "I need more leads" to "I need to stop needing leads." That reframe is the entire wedge.

### Core narrative: "Five Mile Famous"
Anchor every first-touch around recognition, not lead volume: *"Are you the company people already trust before they call, or are you just another name they find when they're already shopping?"* This works because it's a question the prospect hasn't been asked by a marketing vendor before — every other agency pitches leads, rankings, or ad spend efficiency.

### Pain → Message matrix

| Pain (from doc) | Reframe | Opening line pattern |
|---|---|---|
| Rising cost per lead | You're renting attention from Google every month and none of it compounds | "What happens to your lead flow the month you stop paying Google?" |
| Price-shopped, 3–4 competing bids | Customers price-shop because they don't recognize you before they search | "If three other [trade] companies show up next to you on that search, what makes them pick you?" |
| Growth flattened | More ad spend on a flat brand just buys the same plateau at a higher price | "Is more ad spend actually going to move the needle, or just cost more for the same result?" |
| Burned by prior agency (highest-converting signal) | Most agencies sold you lead capture and called it lead generation | "Did your last agency actually build something that outlasted the contract, or just rent you clicks while you paid?" |
| Weak brand recognition / "lead trap" | You're invisible until the exact moment someone's already comparing prices | "Do people in your market know your name, or only find it when they're already shopping three bids?" |

### Hard rules for every touch (from the doc — non-negotiable)
- Never pitch services, technology, pricing, ROI, or guarantees on the call or in copy
- Never commit to a number of leads/calls/booked jobs; never guarantee rankings or CPL
- No "How are you?" / "Is this a good time?" openers
- Vocabulary: **"Brandformance"** (never "brand performance"), **"program"** (never package/plan/retainer), **"lead capture"** (never "lead gen") when referencing paid ads
- Never say "like [competitor] but cheaper" — TopServ is not competing on price, and neither is the outbound motion

### Qualifying signal to listen/read for
The strongest buying signal is *current frustration with an existing marketing vendor* — train the SDR to probe for this early ("Who's currently handling your marketing?") and treat any hesitation or complaint as the number one flag to escalate toward booking, ahead of firmographic fit.

## 4. Phone-First + Email Cadence Design

TopServ's own data says email/LinkedIn-only failed with this audience; phone is primary, email/LinkedIn are support channels that keep the prospect warm between dials and give the SDR a legitimate reason to call back. Design the cadence around the phone, not around email open rates.

### 15-business-day, multi-channel cadence (single SDR capacity: ~350–400 outbound dials/week)

| Day | Channel | Action |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | Phone | Dial 1 (Nooks) — no voicemail on first attempt |
| 1 | Email | Touch 1 — short, curiosity-based, "Five Mile Famous" question, no pitch |
| 2 | Phone | Dial 2, different time-of-day than Dial 1 |
| 3 | LinkedIn | Connection request, no note (avoids looking like a pitch) |
| 4 | Phone | Dial 3 — leave voicemail referencing email sent |
| 5 | Email | Touch 2 — pain-specific (rotate per vertical: CPL for HVAC/plumbing, storm/insurance cycle for roofing, ROI skepticism for solar) |
| 7 | Phone | Dial 4 |
| 8 | LinkedIn | Soft message referencing the voicemail, no ask |
| 9 | Phone | Dial 5 |
| 10 | Email | Touch 3 — "burned by last agency" angle, works as a pattern interrupt this deep in sequence |
| 12 | Phone | Dial 6 |
| 13 | Email | Touch 4 — short breakup email, direct, low-pressure |
| 15 | Phone | Dial 7 (final attempt) — then move to long-term nurture (quarterly re-engagement, not weekly) |

**Design rationale:**
- Phone attempts front-loaded and dense (this audience is reachable but busy — contractors are on job sites, so vary call windows: early morning 7–8am, midday lunch break 11:30–1, and end-of-day 4–6pm)
- Email never asks for a meeting directly until Touch 3+; early touches exist only to make the SDR's next call recognizable ("I sent you a note yesterday about...")
- No pitch content in any channel — every touch is a question or an observation, matching the doc's explicit call-discipline rules
- Suppress anyone matching the 17K GHL CRM export before the list ever reaches the dialer (see Section 5)

## 5. Data & Suppression Strategy

1. **Ingest TopServ's CRM export in the chunks they provide.** Do not wait for the full 17K before starting — request the first chunk (existing clients + anyone already contacted) as a hard suppression list on Day 1.
2. **Build sourced lists by NAICS code + revenue + firmographic filters from Section 2**, using Seamless/Apollo (confirmed NAICS/SIC search works directly in Seamless — see prior reference memory) rather than keyword search, since trade classification by keyword is unreliable for home service verticals.
3. **Cross-reference every new list against the CRM export before dialing** — dedupe on domain + phone, not just email, since contractor businesses often share a generic info@ inbox across multiple lookalike numbers.
4. **Re-run suppression on every subsequent CRM chunk TopServ shares** — this is an ongoing hygiene task, not a one-time setup, and should be logged so both sides can see suppression volume as a trust-building deliverable (shows TopServ their CRM is actually being respected, which matters given the reputational stakes of white-labeling as their own team).
5. **Track a "reason excluded" tag** (existing client / prior contact / below revenue floor / price-led positioning / non-owner-only contact) so early list quality can be tuned per vertical rather than guessed at.

## 6. Objection Handling Integration

The doc already supplies TopServ's approved reframes — the SDR's job is to *not* resolve objections (that's the strategy call's job) but to acknowledge and bridge to the meeting. Build these into the Nooks call script as bridge lines, not close attempts:

| Objection | SDR bridge (not a full rebuttal — that's for the strategy call) |
|---|---|
| "It's too expensive" | "That's fair — worth noting most of what you're already paying Google monthly doesn't accumulate into anything. Ryan/Jonathan walks through the actual numbers on the call." |
| "How many leads will I get?" | "Good question — that's exactly what they'll get specific about on the call. I'm just booking the conversation, not the numbers." |
| "We already rank #1" | "Ranking's visibility — worth 20 minutes to see how that's converting into being the first name people think of, not just the first one they find." |
| "Branding takes too long" | "Understandable — the flip side is what unbranded clicks keep costing month over month. That's the exact tradeoff they unpack on the call." |
| "I got burned by my last agency" | "That's actually the most common reason people end up talking to Jonathan/Ryan — most agencies sold lead capture and called it lead generation. Worth 20 minutes to hear the difference." (**Treat this objection as a green light, not a red one — escalate urgency to book.**) |
| "I don't want to be on camera" | Do not over-engineer on the SDR call — flag it to the closer, don't try to resolve it. "Totally fair, that's something worth raising directly on the call." |
| "I need to think about it" | "Totally — just so I point them the right direction, is it more about timing, budget, or wanting to loop in someone else on the decision?" (qualifies which of the 4 buckets without pushing) |

SDR is never trying to win the objection — only to keep the prospect moving toward the calendar link. This matches the doc's instruction that pitching/pricing/ROI discussion is reserved for the 60-minute strategy call.

## 7. SDR Ramp & Scaling Plan

**Phase 1 — Ramp (Weeks 1–3):** Ship-shape sequences, scripts, and suppressed lists live day 1. First week is volume-building, not results-chasing — expect 30–50% of eventual dial rate while the SDR calibrates on trade-specific language (HVAC vs. roofing vs. solar framing differs enough to matter). Target: 1–2 booked meetings in week 1, ramping to pace for 5/month by end of week 3.

**Phase 2 — Steady state (Month 1–2):** Full cadence live across Tier 1 verticals. Target: 5 meetings booked/month, consistent — this is the scale gate, so treat hitting it twice in a row as the primary KPI above all others in this phase.

**Phase 3 — Scale trigger (after 2 consecutive months at 5+ sales/month — note: confirm with Jonathan/Ryan whether "sales" here means booked meetings or closed deals, since the doc's commercial terms say "5 meetings" for billing start but "5 sales/month" for the scale gate — this is worth clarifying before Month 2 ends):**
- Add SDR #2, split by vertical (e.g., SDR 1 keeps HVAC/plumbing, SDR 2 takes roofing/solar) so each rep develops trade-fluent language rather than diluting across all verticals
- Introduce Tier 2 verticals (electrical, garage doors, windows) once Tier 1 cadence is proven and CRM suppression pipeline is stable
- Begin tracking per-vertical conversion rate (dial → meeting) to decide which verticals earn more SDR capacity going forward

**Reporting cadence:** Weekly dial volume / connect rate / meeting-booked rate to Ryan; monthly rollup to Jonathan tying booked meetings to the 10-new-clients/month stated goal, and flagging any vertical materially over/under-performing.

## 8. Market Dominance Strategy

This is the layer beyond "hit the numbers for TopServ" — it's how this single engagement becomes leverage across TopServ's ecosystem and beyond.

### 8.1 Become the reference case inside Jonathan's ~300-agency network
Jonathan has an existing partner/referral relationship with roughly 300 other agencies. If this engagement performs, TopServ itself becomes a **case study and referral source** for placing the same white-labeled outbound motion inside other brand-first agencies serving other blue-collar/local-service verticals (not just home service — the "own the phone, not email" thesis generalizes to any trade where the buyer answers a phone and doesn't respond to email/LinkedIn: legal, dental, local retail franchises, etc.).
- Ask Jonathan directly, once the scale gate is hit, whether he's open to a warm intro to 2–3 agencies in his network as a referral — this converts one client into a channel.
- Package TopServ's results (meetings booked, verticals proven, objection-to-book conversion) into a reusable "Brandformance-style agency" case study rather than a generic outbound case study — the specificity is what will resonate with agencies running a similar high-ticket, brand-first model.

### 8.2 Apply "Five Mile Famous" logic to the outbound motion itself
TopServ's own positioning — own recognition before the prospect starts shopping — is the same principle that should govern how OSP shows up across TopServ's target verticals. Once Tier 1 (HVAC/roofing/solar/plumbing) is working, the goal isn't just booking meetings — it's building a repeatable playbook of vertical-specific scripts and case evidence ("we've booked X meetings for HVAC companies specifically") that becomes a durable asset TopServ (and by extension OSP) can point to before a prospect ever asks "have you worked with a company like mine?"

### 8.3 White-label integrity as a trust multiplier
Because reps operate as TopServ employees, every professionally-run call *is* TopServ's brand in the market. Treat call quality and CRM hygiene (Section 5) not just as operational tasks but as the proof point that lets TopServ safely expand this motion — sloppy suppression or off-brand call handling directly damages Jonathan's own reputation with his 300-agency network, so the bar here is higher than a typical white-label engagement.

### 8.4 Compounding data asset
Every dial, every objection heard, every "burned by last agency" story collected across hundreds of home service conversations becomes proprietary market intelligence TopServ doesn't get from referrals alone — normalized objection/pain frequency data by vertical, which can eventually inform TopServ's own sales collateral and even product positioning (e.g., if solar consistently surfaces ROI-skepticism at a higher rate than HVAC, that's worth feeding back to Jonathan directly, not just using internally).
